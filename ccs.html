Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)
Pearl of ICT for Development

What is CCS ?

Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) is a technology that can capture up to 90% of the carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions produced from the use of fossil fuels in electricity generation and industrial processes, preventing the carbon dioxide from entering the atmosphere.


Around 40% of global CO2 emissions come from industry. CO2 capture and storage (CCS) can prevent up to 90% of industrial CO2 emissions from reaching the atmosphere. The CO2 emitted by industries such as electricity production, cement manufacture and oil and gas refining can be captured at source, transported, then stored safely and permanently, deep underground. Many of the technologies for CCS already exist and have been used in other industrial processes for many years. A number of large-scale CCS projects are already in operation across the world. The challenge now is to develop CCS for extended large-scale use in power generation and heavy industry.

How does it work ?

The proces of Carbon Capture and Storage consists of 3 parts

Capturing the carbon dioxide

Transporting the carbon dioxide

Storing the carbon dioxide

Capturing the carbon dioxide

For decades, CO2 has been captured from emitting sources and sold for use by other industries, such as soft drink manufacturers. Existing capture technologies can be applied to large-scale industrial processes, such as power generation, oil and gas production and cement manufacture. Capturing CO2requires extra energy, but even taking this into account, the cost efficiency of CCS is still better than most forms of low-carbon energy generation. The main technical challenges now are to continue to address this 'energy penalty' and reduce operating costs. Three main technologies exist: post-combustion; pre-combustion and oxyfiring.

Post-combustion

With post-combustion, the fuel is combusted with air and the CO2 is separated from the exhaust gases after the combustion, typically using liquid solvents. The absorbed CO2 is then removed from the solvent by heating. This technology is already used to capture CO2 for use in the food and beverage industry and as a raw material in fertilizer manufacturing. Post-combustion capture can be retrofitted to existing plants or used in new-build industrial operations. However, the complexity of integrating it into existing plant systems has technical and cost implications, while the need for steam to recover the CO2 can significantly reduce a power plant's generating capacity.

Pre-combustion

Pre-combustion uses steam and air or oxygen to convert fuel into a mixture of mainly hydrogen and CO2. The CO2 is separated using liquid solvents or solid adsorbents while the hydrogen can then be burned without producing further CO2 or used in refinery processes to create cleaner fuels. The pressure and concentration of CO2 obtained through pre-combustion is relatively high, making separation easier to achieve. Pre-combustion has been used for many years in the industrial production of ammonia and hydrogen. However, the fuel conversion steps required are relatively complex, making pre-combustion more suitable for use in new-build plants rather than retrofitting to existing plants.

Oxyfiring

Oxyfiring involves burning fuel in pure oxygen rather than air. This results in an exhaust gas made up largely of CO2 and water vapour. Cooling and compressing this gas stream removes the water vapour and separates out the CO2. A final purification may be needed, depending on the level of impurities in the CO2. One of the benefits of oxyfiring is that very high CO2 capture rates (over 98%) are feasible. The energy penalty in this case is related to the preparation of pure oxygen by air separation. Oxyfiring can be used in new builds and potentially retrofitted to existing plants.

Transporting the carbon dioxide

Captured CO2 has to be compressed and transported, most likely by pipeline, to a site suitable for storage. Ships may be viable for CO2 transportation over long distances. Even if a suitable storage site lies directly beneath the source of capture, the captured CO2 will have to be compressed and pumped underground. Pipelining of gases and liquids is widespread throughout the world, and includes millions of tonnes of CO2 that are transported each year for use in Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR) operations and in emerging CCS projects. Pipeline infrastructure can be costly, but heavy industries are often 'clustered' together which will allow CO2 pipeline networks to be shared.

CO2 Compression

After capture, CO2 is compressed to the point that it becomes very dense like a liquid. The compressed CO2 is then transported by pipeline to the storage site and is compressed further during injection. It increases in density until it reaches a depth of around 1500 meters (5000 feet), after which density becomes constant. In total, the volume of the CO2 decreases by around 99% from capture, meaning considerable amounts of CO2 can be stored in a given site.

Storing the carbon dioxide

The captured, compressed and transported CO2 is injected via wells deep into the Earth's subsurface where it is stored in a near-liquid form in the pores of geological formations, ideally located at depths greater than 800 metres (2600 feet). These rock formations are carefully selected to have appropriate porosity (void space in the rocks) and permeability (allows fluid flow through the rocks), and are overlain by impermeable caprock layers which act as 'seals' to CO2 and brine. At these depths the CO2 remains in a state that has a liquid-like density but a gas-like viscosity, properties which facilitate the injection and movement of CO2 through the rock. In the storage formation, natural trapping processes help retain the CO2 securely and permanently.

Why CCS ?

CCS is one of a suite of technologies that will all be required to combat climate change, including renewables, nuclear and energy efficiency. The importance of CCS as one of the tools against global warming is highlighted in a report by the International Energy Agency, which found that CCS could contribute to a 19% reduction in global CO2 emissions by 2050 and that fighting climate change could cost over 70% more without CCS.
